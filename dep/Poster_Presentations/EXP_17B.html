Synthesis and Purification of Acetylferrocene

Introduction

Ferrocene is an organometallic compound first reported in 1951 and was the first known example of a class of compounds known as sandwich compounds, in reference to the iron(II) cation ”sandwiched” between two aromatic cyclopentadienyl anions. This structure is experimentally supported by the 1H-NMR spectrum of ferrocene. The spectrum (Fig. 8) displays a single peak at 4.17 ppm with an integration value of 10; because all the hydrogen atoms present in the two cyclopentadienyl anions are equivalent, all 10 π orbitals on the ring structures must participate equally in coordination to Fe(II). Ferrocene exhibits chemistry similar to benzene and other aromatic systems, and has been used in polymer synthesis, small medical devices, and as an ”anti-knock” additive in gasoline.3,4


Fig. 1. Reaction scheme and mechanism for the Friedel-Crafts acylation of ferrocene to acetylferrocene. Reproduced from Odinity. 1


Here, ferrocene was acylated via the Friedel-Crafts acylation (Fig. 1) using phosphoric acid as a catalyst; these conditions result in the formation of mono-acetylferrocene as a major product, which was subsequently separated from other components and unwanted byproducts (Fig. 2) in the reaction mixture using flash chromatography.


Fig. 2. Structures of various acetylated ferrocene derivatives. Reproduced from Odinity. 1

Methods

Acylation of Ferrocene

Ferrocene, acetic anhydride, and a catalytic amount of phosphoric acid were combined in a small round-bottom flask fitted with a calcium chloride drying tube (Tab. 1). The flask was swirled in a hot water bath to mix the reagents for 10 minutes. After removing the flask from the heat source, the reaction mixture was analyzed by TLC using a 1:4 ether:hexane solution as solvent. The absence of the bright yellow spot produced by ferrocene confirmed that the reaction had gone to completion. After the reaction flask was allowed to cool for 10 minutes, the reaction mixture was poured over ice in a clean beaker and rinsed with cold water. The acidic components of the solution (H3PO4 catalyst and acetic acid produced as a byproduct) were neutralized by gradual addition of concentrated base. After the resulting mixture had cooled to room temperature, the product was collected by vacuum filtration and dried for 15 minutes under vacuum.

Chromatography

Flash chromatography (medium-pressure chromatography) uses small particles of gel, which restricts the flow of solvent. Thus, pressure – here, a pressure bulb – is applied to drive the solvent through the solid phase. A quality separation depends the absence of air bubbles in the stationary phase; after addition of silica gel, the gel beads were compressed as much as possible by tapping the column and hexanes were run through the solid phase until the gel became fully saturated and homogenous. Care was taken such that the solvent level did not fall beneath the top layer of the gel and that the pressure bulb was removed from the column before release, as either would reintroduce air bubbles into the solid phase. In order to prevent uneven loading of the reaction mixture, a thin layer of sand was packed on top of the silica gel (Fig. 3).


Fig. 3. Figure and photograph of the column setup for separation of ferrocene and its acylated derivatives.

After ferrocene has been completely eluted, a more polar solvent is added (1:4 ethyl acetate: hexanes). This solvent more favorably interacts with (mono-)Acetylferrocene than the gel, which is then collected in a clean beaker. Next, the crude product mixture was transferred to the top of the column and any remaining material was transferred using a minimal amount of clean hexane and pumped into the solid phase. Then, 15 mL of a nonpolar solvent (hexanes) was eluted from the column.

The relatively polar acetylated ferrocene derivatives more favorably interact with the polar silica gel than the nonpolar solvent, and thus remain in the column while any remaining – relatively nonpolar – ferrocene quickly moves through the column and elutes with the solvent. After addition of 10 mL of a more polar solvent (1:1 ether:hexane), an orange-red component – monoacetylferrocene – moved rapidly down the column. When this solution reached the tip of the column, the Fraction 1 receptacle flask was switched with a clean Fraction 2 receptacle flask. Elution continued until the orange-red band had fully eluted from the column. Meanwhile, the much more polar diacetylferrocene still has the strongest interactions with the silica gel, and remained relatively stationary in the column. If one added a more polar solvent, such as a 9:1 ether:hexane solution, it would be possible to elute diacetylferrocene.4

The crude product mixture, Fraction 1, and Fraction 2 were spotted on TLC plates that were run in 1:4 ether:hexane and 1:1 ether:hexane solutions (Fig. 4). As observed in chromatography, ferrocene is the least polar of the three main components of the crude product solution, while diacetylferrocene is the most polar. Thus, ferrocene and diacetylferrocene will travel the longest and shortest distances on the plate, respectively. The solvent in Fraction 2 was evaporated off, and the solid product was allowed to dry for 7 days.


Fig. 4. TLC plates containing the reaction mixture (Lane A), Fraction 1 (ferrocene; Lane B), and Fraction 2 (acetylferrocene; Lane C).

Results and Discussion

The purified product weighed 246.0 mg, a yield of 80.3%, and formed thin orange crystals, as expected (Fig. 5) of ferrocene. The crystals had a melting point range of 80-82 °C, which aligns with the experimental value of 82.5 °C and further confirms that the purification of acetylferrocene from other components of the mixture was successful. Product loss may have occurred when transferring components and products between glassware, especially when washing product from the round-bottom flask. Additionally, reliance only on a visual aid to determine when to switch receptacles and halt collection of the product during chromatography leaves room for error, as the human eye alone is not able to detect small quantities of solvent.


Fig. 5. Purified crystals of acetylferrocene. A: reference image posted by /u/annoclancularius. 2 B: crude product formed from the initial reaction. C/D: photographs of crystals after drying for 7d.

Neither 1H- nor 13C-NMR spectra were taken of the experimental product due to poor resolution of the available instruments. However, these spectra would be quite useful to further confirm that the experimental product was pure acetylferrocene. The 1H- and 13C-NMR spectra of ferrocene each display only one singlet at 4.17 and 68.0 ppm, respectively. When mono-acylated, these peaks shift downfield to 4.20 and 69.0 ppm, respectively. If the synthesized product is impure, the spectra will not display a clear peak at the corresponding chemical shift region. However, if diacetylferrocene is produced and purified, no peaks will appear in these regions.

However, a quick visual examination of the experimental IR spectrum reveals that it (Fig. 6) most closely resembles the reference spectrum of mono-acetylferrocene (Fig. 10). The experimental spectrum does lacks the medium, sharp peak observed at about 1411 cm−1 corresponding to the C=C stretching of ferrocene (Fig. 7), which confirms that any remaining starting material was successfully eluted in the first fraction. Additionally, the strong peak at 1651 cm−1 displayed in the experimental spectrum is represented in both IR spectra of acetyl- and diacetyl-ferrocene, but the peak produced by the product is much weaker than that produced by pure diacetylferrocene (Fig. 13); overall, this indicates that the synthesis of acetylferrocene by Friedel-Crafts acylation of ferrocene, and subsequent purification of acetylferrocene from unreacted starting material and unwanted byproducts was successful.
